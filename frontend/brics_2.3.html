South Africa Chinese Investment Ahead of BRICS Summit

South Africa is seeking more Chinese investments as it struggles with the impact of rolling blackouts and high unemployment.

Chinese President Xi Jinping is expected to lead a large business delegation to South Africa later next month when he attends the annual summit of the emerging markets group BRICS, which also includes Brazil, India and Russia, in Johannesburg.

Fikile Majola, South Africa’s deputy minister of trade and industry, spoke to a business seminar in Beijing via video link, saying the country was looking to improve infrastructure.

“Despite South Africa’s success over the years, our country is facing several challenges that need our immediate attention to overcome and I wish to assure you that we are determined to change this situation for the better,” Majola said.

“We are well aware that we must prioritise economic infrastructure, especially scaling up renewable energy capacity, roads and railways, ports, and airports, telecommunications and water infrastructure.”

The country is struggling to produce enough energy – partly as a result of the breakdown of ageing coal-fired power plants and mismanagement at the state power company Eskom – as well as suffering from the impact of rising food and fuel prices driven by Russia’s war in Ukraine.

Last month, the IMF warned that “reforms were urgently needed” to resolve the energy crisis and tackle structural unemployment, which stood at 32.9 per cent, close to an all-time high, in the first quarter of the year.

There have been concerns that the country’s public debt levels – the highest among emerging markets – are holding back the government’s ability to respond to shocks and meet growing social and development needs.

The crisis deepened last week when hundreds of members of the country’s largest trade union gathered in Johannesburg to demand interest rate cuts, electricity reforms and more jobs.

Majola said his country was also looking to use Chinese expertise in fields such as new energy, mining and metal processing.

China is the world’s leading clean energy producer, generating 1,213 gigawatts from renewable sources last year, in contrast to South Africa’s total of 10,445 megawatts

“Today, South Africa has potential to attract and implement renewable energy projects,” Majola told the event in Beijing. “South Africa’s growing energy requirements provide lucrative investment opportunities across numerous energy value chains.”

The country also has the world’s largest reserves of platinum group metals, which play a vital role in hydrogen production and fuel cells for electric vehicles.

South Africa launched a national platinum strategy in 2019 which aims to create a million jobs and contribute the equivalent of US$441 billion to South Africa’s economy by 2050.

South Africa is China’s largest trading partner in Africa and the launch of the African Continental Free Trade Area – the world’s largest free trade area connecting 1.3 billion people across 55 countries – in 2021 has raised hopes that China can use the country as a springboard to further develop its presence across the continent.

Busi Mabuza, chairwoman of the South Africa BRICS Business Council, told the event in Beijing that better cross-border infrastructure was key, adding: “[It is] very, very important to unlock that in order for intra-Africa trade to actually become a reality.

“And we believe that in China, we have a partner with the means and the solutions to assist us in getting that cross-border infrastructure on the ground.”

Siyabonga Cwele, South Africa’s ambassador to China, said on the sidelines of the Beijing seminar that despite their different political systems, the BRICS member states were united in wanting “a fairer global trading system, fairer representation of developing nations, and more inclusive [global] financial institutions”.

Cwele also said the New Development Bank – established by the BRICS states in 2015 to offer alternatives to dollar-denominated lending – is likely to offer proposals for an “alternative currency for international trade” to offset pressures from“unilateral sanctions”.

However, Cwele said the currency was designed to provide “alternative financial arrangements” to overcome a range of challenges rather than compete with the US dollar.

He added that Western sanctions on Russia are “unilateral sanctions, they are not UN sanctions and these sanctions are affecting us in Africa in a very large way, the food prices, the oil prices and the inflationary pressure we’re all facing”.
